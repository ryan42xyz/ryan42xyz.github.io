Question: If AI Can Write Blogs, Why Write Yourself?

AI can write well. Give it a topic, and it can generate articles with clear structure, smooth logic, and even good prose. So why write yourself?

The answer depends on your objective function.

If your goal is content production‚Äîdisseminating information, gaining exposure, SEO rankings, reader count‚Äîthen AI can indeed help you complete most of the work. You only need to provide direction; AI handles generation, optimization, and distribution.

But if your goal in writing blogs is capability improvement and decision quality, the situation is different.

AI-generated articles may look good, but you may have only ‚Äúcompleted an article‚Äù rather than ‚Äúformed a capability.‚Äù Understanding doesn‚Äôt mean you can invoke it in real situations. Remembering doesn‚Äôt mean you can do it right under pressure.

When your goal is capability/decision models, AI can produce content for you, but it‚Äôs hard for it to form capabilities for you.

Core Assertion

Capabilities are not formed by understanding; they are formed by invocation.

This is like the difference between code and documentation. AI can help you write documentation, telling you ‚Äúhow to use this function,‚Äù but it cannot give you the ability to write code. You can understand documentation, but if you haven‚Äôt written code yourself, you still can‚Äôt write.

Writing is the same. AI can generate an article about ‚Äúhow to make decisions,‚Äù telling you the steps and principles of decision-making. But if you haven‚Äôt experienced the process of reasoning, counterexamples, and trade-offs, you‚Äôll find it hard to invoke this knowledge in real situations.

Capability formation requires three levels:

  1. Text Model (AI/article): Structures written on paper that you can understand
  2. Conceptual Model (you restate/question): You restate in your own words, find counterexamples, question boundaries
  3. Behavioral Model (real-world invocation): You can automatically invoke and do it right under pressure

AI excels at generating text models but cannot help you cross from text models to behavioral models. This crossing must be done by you personally‚Äîthrough reasoning, counterexamples, application, mistakes, and corrections.

When your goal is capability/decision models, you must retain the ‚Äúreasoning and guarantee‚Äù part. AI can produce content for you, but reasoning and trade-offs must be done by you.

Boundary Statement

This article discusses a specific writing goal: writing that compresses experience into reusable decision models.

This is not a general guide on ‚Äúhow to write blogs.‚Äù I‚Äôm discussing ‚Äúcapability-formation writing,‚Äù not all blogs.

Applicable scenarios:

  • Your writing goal is: capability improvement / decision quality improvement / compound cognitive assets
  • You hope the article will leave behind: checklist / decision tree / runbook / review templates

Not forcibly covering:

  • Pure recording type (life diaries, fragmentary essays)
  • Pure dissemination type (news summaries, reposts)
  • Pure resonance type (emotion/narrative-focused)
  • Pure teaching type (step-by-step for beginners)

I‚Äôm not saying these have no value. I‚Äôm just saying: if your goal is ‚Äúreusable decision capabilities,‚Äù you need to use a different standard.

For example: generic tutorials have decreasing marginal value, but ‚Äúcounterintuitive tutorials/decision tutorials after pitfalls‚Äù are still valuable. The difference is: whether it compresses experience into models, rather than just restating steps.

Human-AI Division of Labor

Since AI can produce content but cannot form capabilities for you, how to divide labor?

I provide an actionable model: Type A/B error classification.

Type A errors: Errors that change action choices (making you do wrong things/things you shouldn‚Äôt do)

  • Must be handled by humans: position, causality, boundaries, evidence, final conclusions

Type B errors: Errors that don‚Äôt change action choices (only affect reading experience/efficiency)

  • Can be delegated to AI: structure, wording, examples, titles, polishing, summaries

Gray zone judgment rule: If an ‚Äúexpression/structure‚Äù causes boundary conditions to be omitted, making readers act differently, it‚Äôs Type A.

Risk Warning

The biggest risk is thinking illusion: completion feeling ‚â† ownership.

AI-generated articles may give you the illusion that ‚ÄúI‚Äôve already thought about this.‚Äù You understand, remember, even can restate, but if you haven‚Äôt experienced the process of reasoning, counterexamples, and trade-offs, you‚Äôll find it hard to invoke in pressure situations.

How to identify thinking illusion?

If two weeks later you cannot invoke it in real situations, it‚Äôs more likely a completed article rather than formed capability.

Internalization has three levels:

  • Level 1: Need prompts to recall (also valuable, suitable for short-term or low-frequency knowledge)
  • Level 2: Can restate core mechanisms (suitable for medium-frequency reuse)
  • Level 3: Can automatically invoke and do right in real situations (the compound asset you pursue)

If you‚Äôre only Level 1 or Level 2, you may have only ‚Äúcompleted an article‚Äù rather than ‚Äúformed a capability.‚Äù

AI Collaboration Framework

Since AI has value but cannot form capabilities for you, how to collaborate?

Core principle: AI output is untrusted by default, only as candidates; must pass validation threshold to enter main text.

AI generates candidate model drafts, not directly usable capability models.

AI‚Äôs value:

  • Provide candidates (divergent generation)
  • Provide attacks (adversarial auditing)
  • Provide compression (structural modeling)
  • Save time (expression optimization)

But: Final model ownership must be with humans. AI can generate text models, but humans must be responsible for training text models into behavioral models (through counterexamples, restatement, application).

AI‚Äôs failure modes:

The crossing from text model to behavioral model takes time: text model ‚Üí conceptual model ‚Üí behavioral model. This process cannot be accelerated; it can only be completed through practice.

Implementation Loop

How to turn articles into reusable decision models?

Decision model structure (minimum viable template):

  1. Context/Trigger Conditions: Under what circumstances it applies
  2. Conclusion/Action: What decision should be made
  3. Constraints/What Not to Do: Environmental constraints, boundary conditions, what not to do
  4. Risk and Reversibility: How costly if wrong, can it roll back
  5. Update Signals: What signs indicate need for correction

This is not ‚Äúminimum complete,‚Äù but ‚Äúminimum viable.‚Äù The most practical template for engineering/career decisions.

Review loop:

  • Two weeks later review: Was this model invoked in real situations? How effective?
  • Update trigger: What new situations appeared? Which dimensions need updating?
  • Update record: Record update reason, update content, post-update validation

This is the ideal loop; in practice it can be simplified. The key is: turn articles into executable assets (checklists, decision trees, runbooks), not just leave an article.
